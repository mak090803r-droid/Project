Reading a complete translated page as uninterrupted speech creates a serious usability problem. The listener hears the meaning but cannot tell which original line or paragraph is currently being spoken This becomes especially confusing on technical documents containing warnings, numbered procedures. labels, and tables. A wearable translator should preserve the connection between physical text and audio. The processing pipeline therefore retains the coordinates returned by optical character recognition and groups nearby lines into meaningful regions. Each region becomes a separate translation unit instead of sending the entire page to the language model at once. Reading order is determined from position, alignment, and spacing Lines that share a similar left edge and remain within a reasonable vertical distance are combined Large gaps. headings, bullet points, and changes in indentation begin new groups The original coordinates remain attached to every translatedsentenceDuring development,theselected paragraph is surrounded by a coloured box in the preview In the wearable version, a short tone and a spoken paragraph number announce the same transition. The user can move forward, go back, repeat, pause, or stop with a small physical control or a limited voice command set. Speech is generated only after a paragraph passes basic checks. Empty results and very low recognition confidence request another image. Extremely long regions are divided at sentence boundaries, while short neighbouring lines may be joined so the voice does not pause after every label fragment. Numbers, units, dates, and warning symbols receive special attention because a fluent sentence can still be dangerous when one measurement is wrong. The interface can optionally read the source paragraph first and the translated paragraph second. This slower mode helps during evaluation and allows bilingual users to notice recognition mistakes before acting on the result.
