Autonomous Monitoring of a Remote Hyb-Energy Station An isolated plateau station powers a medical clinic, radio repeater, weather instruments, and emergency lighting. Its microgrid combines solar panels, lithium batteries, and a compact diesel generator. Because winter roads may remain closed for days, a local controller samples voltage, current, temperature, and vibration every 200 milliseconds. Detailed records stay on site, while compressed summaries are transmitted whenever the link becomes available. Medical and communications loads always receive priority On 18 October 2025, a dust storm reduced solar generation by 34 percent in fifteen minutes. Battery-cabinet temperature increased by 8 °C, and the inverter fan developed an unusual low-frequency vibration. Rather than shut down the station, the controller disconnected secondary heaters, reduced selected lighting, and started the backup generator. Diagnostic software separated the incident into three likely faults: debris on the solar array, wear in a fan bearing. and rising contact resistance at a battery connector. After the storm, a head-mounted camera read equipment labels and alarm codes, which a local processor compared with an offline manual Sensitive images were not uploaded to the cloud The capture program required the complete label to remain inside a safe border and checked sharpness across the full frame and its upper region. Spoken guidance requested a step backward when an edge was missing, and four stable observations were required. Engineers later confirmed the suggested order clear the panels, replace the bearing, then tighten the connector. The six-minute repair caused no critical interruption. Trustworthy automation must expose uncertainty and leave final responsibility with the human operator
